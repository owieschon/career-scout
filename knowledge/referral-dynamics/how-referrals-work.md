# How Referrals Actually Work

<!-- clean-docs:purpose -->
*A referral is not "a friend forwarding your resume." A referral is a specific ATS event tied to a specific employee with a written endorsement. The difference between a real referral and a forwarded resume is roughly a 5-10x conversion rate.*

## The ATS Mechanics

Every modern ATS (Greenhouse, Lever, Workday, Ashby, Workable, iCIMS) has a structured referral flow. When an employee submits a referral, the system records:

- **Referral source tag**: usually a dropdown the candidate sees on the application ("How did you hear about us?") plus a backend flag set when an employee submits via the employee portal
- **Referrer name**: the specific employee who submitted
- **Referral channel**: portal submission vs employee survey vs "I know this person" added later
- **Endorsement text**: free-form notes the employee writes about why they're recommending the candidate
- **Date stamp**: critical for the referral bonus eligibility window

There is a real, documented distinction in the ATS between:

1. **Submitted by employee** (employee logged into the referral portal, submitted the candidate with their info pre-attached). This is a Tier 1 referral.
2. **Applied via referral link** (employee shared a unique link, candidate clicked through and applied). This is Tier 2.
3. **Applied + employee added later** ("I know this person, you should look at them" added as a note after the candidate applied independently). This is Tier 3.
4. **Applied + employee mentioned in cover letter** (no portal action at all). This is Tier 4 and barely counts as a referral.

The conversion rate gap between Tier 1 and Tier 4 is large. A Tier 1 referral with a strong written endorsement typically gets HM screen at 30-60% rate at well-run companies. A Tier 4 "mentioned in cover letter" referral converts at roughly the same rate as a cold application, sometimes worse because it looks like name-dropping.

## Referral Bonuses

Standard structure across mid-to-large companies:

- **Range**: $1,000 to $10,000 for individual contributor roles. $5,000 is a rough median for tech roles. Engineering and senior IC roles trend toward $5K-$10K. Sales/CS roles trend toward $1K-$5K.
- **Payout timing**: typically 50% at start date, 50% at 6-month mark. Some companies pay the full amount at 90 days. A few hold all of it until 1 year.
- **Diversity multipliers**: some companies (still common in 2024-2026) pay 2x or more for referrals of underrepresented candidates. This is usually visible in the employee handbook but not advertised externally.
- **Hard-to-fill role multipliers**: senior engineering, AI/ML, security, and specialized GTM roles often have inflated bonuses ($10K-$25K range). These are usually time-limited.
- **Ineligibility**: the employee usually has to be in good standing, not on a PIP, and not in the same reporting chain as the referred role.

Some startups (under 100 people) don't have formal referral programs at all. At that stage, the founder or CEO knows everyone, and "referrals" are just trusted introductions. The hiring lift from a founder intro is much larger than any bonus structure could replicate.

## How Referrals Get Prioritized

The standard flow at a well-run company:

1. Cold application enters the pipeline. Goes into recruiter queue. Gets reviewed in batch, often skimmed in 10-15 seconds.
2. Referral (Tier 1) enters the pipeline. Triggers an automatic notification or flag for the recruiter. Many companies have a policy that referrals get a human review within 48 hours.
3. At well-run companies, strong-endorsement referrals (the employee wrote 2+ sentences) often skip the recruiter resume review entirely and go straight to the HM as a "please screen this person."

The reality is messier:

- Some recruiters reject referrals if the candidate doesn't meet the JD's stated requirements, same as any cold app. The endorsement gets read but doesn't override "no 5+ years."
- Some HMs ignore referrals from employees they don't respect. An IC referral from someone known to be a poor judge of talent doesn't get the same lift.
- Referrals from executives or founders almost always get a screen. Even a weak endorsement from a VP usually buys a 30-minute conversation.
- Referrals from the actual hiring manager's direct team almost always get a screen. "My senior eng wants to talk to this person" carries enormous weight.

## Warm vs Cold Referrals

The hierarchy of referral strength, roughly:

1. **The HM's direct report submits with strong endorsement.** Near-automatic screen. 60-80% screen-to-hire is plausible.
2. **A senior IC or manager on the team submits with strong endorsement.** Strong lift. 40-60% to screen.
3. **An employee at the company (different team) submits with strong endorsement.** Solid lift. 30-50% to screen.
4. **An employee submits with minimal endorsement** ("I know this person from a previous company"). Modest lift. 15-30% to screen, depending on the company's referral culture.
5. **Friend forwards your resume to a hiring manager directly via email/Slack.** Highly variable. Can be very strong (if the friend is trusted by the HM) or zero (if the friend is just a name in the company directory).
6. **Cover letter mentions an employee.** Almost zero lift. Often hurts because it looks transactional.

The single biggest determinant of referral strength is whether the referring employee has a track record at the company. A 6-month employee referring people gets less lift than a 4-year employee referring people. The system has memory.

## How to Get a Referral From Someone You Don't Know Well

This is the most common case and the most botched.

**The wrong way (kills conversion):**
- LinkedIn cold message: "Hi, I noticed you work at X. I'm applying for a role there. Would you be willing to refer me?"
- Sending the resume immediately
- Asking on the first interaction
- Vague: "I'd love to learn about your experience there" with a clear bait-and-switch

**The right way:**

1. **Find a non-resume reason to talk.** A genuine question about their work, a comment on something specific they wrote or built, a connection through someone you both know. The first interaction is not about the job.

2. **Earn the right to ask.** Either through one substantive exchange (a 20-minute call where you brought value, not just questions) or through clear shared context (worked at the same company, both did something specific).

3. **Make the ask precise and easy.** When you do ask, send them:
   - The specific role link
   - A 3-bullet summary of why you'd be a strong candidate, written so they can paste it into the referral portal endorsement field
   - One sentence about the gap (the part of the JD that's a stretch) so they know what they're vouching for
   - An explicit out: "If this isn't a fit or you don't have enough context on me, no problem at all. Happy to hear that as a yes or a no."

4. **Tell them what kind of referral you're asking for.** Tier 1 (portal submission) is a real ask. Tier 3 (just adding a note after I apply) is a lighter ask. Be explicit so they know what you want and they can choose.

The single line that lifts conversion: "I'm not asking you to vouch for me beyond what you actually know. If the right framing is 'we worked together briefly and they seemed sharp,' that's totally fine. I'd rather have an honest light endorsement than an inflated one."

## What Makes Referrers Say Yes

People say yes to referrals when:
- The risk-to-reward is asymmetric in their favor (low downside if you don't get hired, real upside in the bonus and the social credit)
- The candidate has done their homework and isn't going to embarrass them
- The candidate has made the work easy (drafted endorsement, clear next steps)
- The role is one the referrer doesn't have to defend (a clear fit, not a stretch they have to justify)

People say no, or ghost, when:
- The ask is the first interaction
- The candidate is asking for help on a role that's a clear mismatch
- The candidate seems desperate or pressuring
- The referrer doesn't actually have enough context to vouch and the candidate didn't acknowledge that

## What NOT to Do

- **Don't ask for a referral as the first message.** This is the number one mistake. It signals you see the person as a means to an end.
- **Don't ask the wrong person.** A junior IC in a different org from your target role can't help you. Find the right person: someone on the target team, or in an adjacent function who'd plausibly know the HM.
- **Don't ghost after the referral.** If they submitted you and you got rejected, tell them. If you got the interview, tell them. If you got the offer, tell them and thank them properly. People remember.
- **Don't ask multiple people at the same company.** Pick one. Multiple referrals from different people for the same candidate often gets flagged as a candidate who's "shopping the company" rather than genuinely interested.
- **Don't ask for the referral and then negotiate hard against the company.** The referrer's reputation is on the line. Make them look good through the process.
- **Don't ask for a referral when you haven't applied yet.** Apply first, then ask the employee to submit a referral note linking to your existing application. This makes the referral concrete and easy.

## Startup Dynamics

At companies under 100 people, the referral dynamic flips:

- **Founder/exec referrals are dispositive.** If the CEO says "talk to this person," there's a screen. Almost no exceptions.
- **There may be no referral portal at all.** The "referral" is a Slack message: "I want to bring in [name] for [role], here's their resume."
- **Bonuses may not exist.** Or they may be ad-hoc and negotiated case by case.
- **The risk of being seen as "the founder's friend" is real.** If a founder pushes you in, the team may resent it. You have to win them in the interview, not just clear the bar.
- **First-degree network is everything.** At seed and Series A, most hires come from the founder's direct network. A warm intro from someone in the founder's circle is more valuable than any cold application volume.

## The "Hiring Manager Direct Outreach" Variant

Adjacent to referrals: messaging the HM directly, bypassing the referral system. This works when:
- The HM is public-facing (active on LinkedIn, Twitter, podcasts)
- You have something specific and useful to say (not "I'd love to chat")
- You can demonstrate the work in the message itself (a small artifact, a sharp take on their problem)

This is not a referral but it functions similarly: it pulls you out of the cold-application pile and into a human conversation. The conversion rate from a well-crafted HM direct message to a screen can rival a Tier 1 referral.

## What This Means for the Operator

- **Identify one well-connected employee per target company, not three.** A focused, real relationship with one person who can submit a Tier 1 referral beats six LinkedIn cold messages.
- **At AI-native startups (Track 4), founder/exec intros are the real game.** A Tier 1 referral from a senior IC at a 20-person company is meaningful, but a direct intro from someone in the founder's circle is 10x more valuable. Spend cycles on the latter.
- **The Cadence Analytics work is the best referral-earning asset.** When asking for a referral from a stranger, lead with "I built a B2B revenue intelligence platform end-to-end, happy to walk you through what I learned about [their domain]." That's a real reason for them to talk to you, not a transactional ask.
- **Draft the endorsement for the referrer.** Three bullets they can paste verbatim into the portal. This is the single highest-use move and almost no one does it.
- **Track every referral ask and outcome.** Who asked, when, outcome, whether they need a thank-you note. Referrers remember being thanked and remember being ghosted in equal measure.
