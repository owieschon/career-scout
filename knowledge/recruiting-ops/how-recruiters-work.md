# How Recruiters Actually Work

*Notes from inside the recruiting function. Most candidates misread the incentives and the workload, which is why their outreach gets ignored and their applications die in the queue. This document explains what a recruiter's day actually looks like, who they prioritize, and why.*

## The Sourcing Pipeline

Most candidates assume recruiters find people primarily through the application pile. That's backwards for any role above SDR level. The pile is the bottom of the funnel, not the top.

A typical full-cycle recruiter at a B2B SaaS company runs four sourcing channels in parallel:

1. **LinkedIn Recruiter (LIR).** The paid tool, not the consumer version. Recruiters build Boolean searches with title strings, skills, company filters, and "Open to Work" signals. A common AE search string looks like: `("Account Executive" OR "Senior AE" OR "Enterprise AE") AND ("manufacturing" OR "industrial" OR "supply chain") AND NOT ("SDR" OR "BDR")` with location and tenure filters layered on. LIR also surfaces "recommended matches" via its own ML model, which is mediocre but determines who appears at the top of the recruiter's daily queue.
2. **Referrals.** Internal referral pipelines convert at 3-5x the rate of cold applicants and are usually the first place a recruiter looks. Most companies pay $1K-$5K referral bonuses (sometimes $10K+ for senior engineering). Recruiters know this and aggressively work their employees' networks before posting externally.
3. **Talent CRM / past pipelines.** Tools like Gem, Beamery, hireEZ, and Greenhouse's built-in talent pool let recruiters re-engage candidates from previous searches. If you made it to an onsite a year ago and got passed over for "experience," you're probably tagged for re-engagement.
4. **Inbound applications.** The pile. Usually the last channel a senior recruiter actually mines, and often only after the funnel from the first three has been exhausted.

For roles like Forward Deployed Engineer or Sr Solutions Engineer at hot AI companies (Anthropic, OpenAI, Hex, Octave AI, Cascade AI), the inbound pile can be 500-2000 applicants per req. The recruiter is not reading those resumes one by one. They're skimming the top 50 by ATS rank and source channel.

## The Screening Workflow

Once a candidate enters the pipeline (whether through outreach response or application), the workflow is roughly:

1. **Resume review.** 30-90 seconds. The recruiter is looking for company brand recognition, title trajectory, tenure stability, and 3-5 skill keywords matching the JD. They are not reading bullet points carefully.
2. **Recruiter phone screen.** 25-30 minutes. Goals: confirm comp expectations match band, confirm location/work auth/visa, surface any obvious red flags (gaps, short stints, role mismatch), and gauge whether the candidate is "sticky" enough to invest the next 6 hours of process time in.
3. **Recruiter-to-Hiring-Manager handoff.** Recruiter writes a short summary in the ATS (3-5 bullets) and submits to HM. HM decides yes/no on first-round interview within 24-72 hours, sometimes longer.
4. **HM screen.** 30-45 minutes. This is the first real signal call.

The recruiter phone screen is where most candidates lose. They treat it like a casual chat. It is not. It is a structured filter. The recruiter has a checklist and is mentally scoring you against the bar set by the HM.

## Recruiter Incentives (This Is Everything)

The single most important thing to understand: recruiters are paid differently depending on their org model, and that changes who they push.

- **Agency contingent recruiters.** Paid 20-25% of first-year base salary, only if their candidate gets hired. They will spam-submit candidates. They will not invest more than 15 minutes per candidate unless that candidate is clearly closeable. They juggle 20-40 active candidates across 5-10 clients. Optimize for volume.
- **Agency retained recruiters.** Paid up-front for executive search, usually 30-33% of total comp. Higher quality bar, longer relationships, but the role floor is typically VP+. The operator is below their typical range right now.
- **Internal corporate recruiters.** Salaried, often with quarterly hiring quotas (e.g., "fill 8 reqs this quarter"). They get a small bonus for hitting target. Their incentive is to close *any* qualified candidate fast, not necessarily the best candidate. They juggle 8-20 open reqs at once.
- **Embedded RPO (Recruitment Process Outsourcing).** External recruiters embedded inside the company. Common at high-growth startups (Series B-D) who can't afford to build an internal team. They behave like internal recruiters but rotate companies every 6-18 months. Lower investment in long-term candidate relationships.

The implication: when an internal recruiter at a 200-person company tells you "we're still considering candidates," they often mean "the HM hasn't decided and I have 11 other reqs to fill this quarter." The delay is usually not about you.

## Req Load and Time-to-Fill Pressure

Industry benchmarks (SHRM, LinkedIn Talent Solutions):

- Average internal recruiter carries **15-25 open reqs** at peak. At high-growth startups it spikes to 30+.
- **Time-to-fill** averages 36-44 days for most B2B roles, 50-70 days for engineering/sales-leadership.
- **Time-to-hire** (from first contact to accepted offer) averages 24-30 days for the candidate who actually gets hired.

A recruiter has roughly **15-20 minutes per req per day** in practice once you net out interviews, debriefs, sourcing, scheduling, and HM 1:1s. That's why fast-moving, easy-to-close candidates get prioritized over candidates who look slightly better on paper but require more handholding.

## Who Gets Prioritized

A recruiter's mental ranking, roughly in order:

1. Referred candidates (already pre-vetted by an employee).
2. Candidates they've worked with before from old pipelines.
3. Candidates with strong brand-name companies on their resume in the same vertical.
4. Inbound applicants who match the JD keywords and show clear title-level fit.
5. Outbound sourced candidates from LIR (response rate to cold InMail is 15-25% for good roles).
6. Everyone else.

A candidate moves up the stack by being **easy to close**: clear on comp, clear on location, available to interview quickly, responsive within 24 hours, no obvious friction (visa, relocation, notice period beyond 4 weeks).

## What Makes a Candidate "Sticky"

Sticky candidates get talked about in debriefs, get pushed by the recruiter to the HM, and get the recruiter to advocate for a stretch on the comp band. Forgettable candidates get the polite-decline template.

Sticky signals:

- A specific, concrete story tied to the company's problem space, told in the first 5 minutes of the screen
- A clear narrative for why *this* role at *this* company (not generic "I'm passionate about your mission")
- Asking 2-3 sharp questions about the team, the metric the role owns, the runway, or the failure modes
- Following up within 24 hours with a specific reference to something in the conversation
- Being clear about comp expectations without being defensive or evasive

Forgettable signals: vague answers, hedging on comp, complaining about previous employers, "tell me more about the role" without having read the JD, asking about benefits before the second interview.

## How to Actually Reach a Flooded Recruiter

Cold InMail and email response rates for candidates contacting recruiters cold are brutal, typically under 5%. The asymmetry is real: recruiters InMail candidates; candidates InMailing recruiters is usually noise.

What actually works:

1. **Get referred.** A short note from a current employee saying "this person is worth 15 minutes" jumps the queue immediately.
2. **Reach the hiring manager directly, not the recruiter.** HMs have authority. Recruiters have process. A short, specific note to the HM on LinkedIn or via found email that demonstrates you understand their actual problem will sometimes route back to the recruiter as "please get this person on the calendar."
3. **If you must contact the recruiter, be transactional.** Subject line: the req ID or exact job title. Body: 3 sentences. Sentence 1: what role, what company, where you saw it. Sentence 2: the single most relevant thing about you (not your whole story). Sentence 3: a specific ask ("would 15 minutes this week or next make sense?"). No attachments. No long pitch.
4. **Time of day matters.** Tuesday-Thursday, 8-10am or 1-3pm in the recruiter's timezone. Mondays are buried in inbox triage. Fridays die.

## What This Means for the Operator

- For Tracks 1, 2, 3 (AE, RevOps, TAM), assume the recruiter is juggling 15-20 reqs. Make their job easier: be crisp on comp (an example band with a clear preferred range), confirm remote-first preference and Columbus OH location up front, and surface the manufacturing/industrial credibility (Lattice Additive, desktop 3D printing, a major aerospace OEM / defense prime customer base) in the first paragraph of any outreach.
- For Track 4 (Forward Deployed / Applied AI), the inbound pile is brutal. Skip it. Go direct to the hiring manager or get referred. The Cadence Analytics build + production ML work is exactly the kind of sticky signal that gets talked about in debriefs, but only if a human actually sees the resume.
- Cold applying to AI-native companies via the careers page is the slowest path. Budget no more than 30 minutes per application there, and invest the rest in HM outreach and referrals.
- A remote-first preference is a "make me close-able" detail for the recruiter. Surface it at the right moment (after first HM screen, before onsite) so the recruiter can advocate or de-prioritize without surprise at offer stage.
- Track which recruiters are agency vs internal in the pipeline DB. Agency recruiters will move you faster but their bar is lower and the role quality is uneven. Internal recruiters at companies you actually want are worth the longer cycle.
