# What Hiring Managers Actually Evaluate

## The Posting Is Not the Job

A job description is almost never written by the hiring manager from scratch. The usual workflow: HR pulls the last person's JD (often from 2-3 years ago), the HM redlines it in 20 minutes between meetings, recruiting adds boilerplate, legal adds disclaimers. By the time it posts, the HM may not have read the final version.

What this means in practice:
- The "5+ years of X" line is often a copy from the prior posting or a guess. Many HMs will hire 3 years of strong X over 7 years of mediocre X.
- The skills list is aspirational. Half of it is "would be nice."
- The seniority title was negotiated with HR/comp, not the HM. A "Senior" title with a $130K band means something very different at a Series B than at Salesforce.
- The "must have" vs "nice to have" split is often wrong. The HM has one or two actual deal-breakers, and they are not always written down.

Best signal of what the HM actually wants: read the LinkedIn profile of the person who last held the role. That is what the HM thought "good" looked like at hire.

## The Four Real Questions

When an HM reads a resume or sits down for a 30-minute screen, they are scoring four things, mostly subconsciously:

1. **Can you do the job on day 1?** Not "do you have potential." Can you walk in Monday morning and not need 6 weeks of ramp on basic mechanics. This is the bar for IC hires at companies with no patience for ramp.
2. **Can you grow with the role?** Will you still be useful 18 months from now when the company is 2x the size. This is where "trajectory" beats "credentials." An AE who has gone from $80K to $200K quota in 3 years beats one who has been at $200K quota for 5 years.
3. **Will the team want you in the room?** Not "are you nice." Will the senior IC who has to onboard you find you annoying. Will the eng team take you seriously. Will the CEO trust you in a customer escalation.
4. **Can I trust you to ship?** Translation: will you be self-directed, will you tell me when something is broken, will you do what you said you'd do without being chased.

Everything else is downstream of these four.

## How HMs Read a Resume

The first pass is 20-40 seconds. Most HMs scan in this order:

1. **Title trajectory** (left margin, top half): are the titles going up, sideways, or down. Sideways is fine. Down or stuck is a flag.
2. **Company names**: brand recognition matters more than HMs admit. A major aerospace OEM or a defense prime in the work history opens doors that "regional manufacturer" does not, even if the actual work was identical.
3. **Tenure pattern**: less than 18 months at multiple roles is a flag. The HM is asking "will this person leave in a year." Two short stints in a row needs an explanation.
4. **Numbers in bullets**: HMs look for dollar amounts, percentages, growth multiples. "Led customer success" is invisible. "Grew $4M book to $7M, 92% NRR" gets read.

What HMs ignore on resumes:
- Skills lists (treated as candidate-asserted, low signal)
- Certifications (Salesforce admin certs etc. are checkboxes, not differentiators)
- Education for anyone 5+ years out of school
- "Objective" or "Summary" paragraphs (most are skipped entirely)
- Tools/stack lists unless the role is deeply technical

What gets read:
- Bullets with numbers
- The "why did they leave" implicit story between roles
- The most recent 2-3 years in detail, earlier roles in skim

## The Truth About "Culture Fit"

"Culture fit" is the legal-safe phrase for a real thing the HM is evaluating, but it's also the catch-all bucket for bias. Here's how it actually works inside the HM's head:

The genuine question is: "Will this person make my team better or worse to work with." That breaks down into:
- Do they take feedback without becoming defensive
- Are they low-drama in conflict
- Do they have opinions but hold them loosely
- Will they raise their hand when stuck instead of silently failing

The illegitimate version that creeps in: are they like me, do they remind me of the last good hire, do they look like the team. Good HMs flag this in themselves. Many do not.

For candidates, the practical implication: "culture fit" loss is rarely about being "not a fit." It is usually about a specific moment in the interview where the HM thought "this person seems hard to work with" or "this person needs a lot of reassurance." Those moments are recoverable if you spot them.

## The Recruiter Handoff

HMs trust some recruiters and ignore others. The trust is built on past hires that worked out. A new recruiter or one with a track record of "presenting candidates who don't make it past round 2" gets ignored on their summaries.

What HMs actually read in the recruiter's submission packet:
- The resume (always)
- The recruiter's 2-3 sentence summary IF they trust the recruiter
- The compensation expectations (always, even if no one admits it)
- The "why this candidate is interested in this role" line (treated skeptically, since candidates often say what recruiters coach them to say)

What HMs ignore:
- Long recruiter summaries
- "Cultural fit" assertions from recruiters
- Skill ratings on scorecards
- LinkedIn AI match percentages

## What Makes an HM Excited vs Cautious in 30 Minutes

Excited signals:
- The candidate has a specific, non-generic reason for being interested in this company (not "I love the mission")
- They ask sharp, informed questions about the team, the product, the current bottleneck
- They tell a story about a hard moment that they handled, with specifics, and don't dodge their own role in it
- They have a point of view on the role or the function that differs from the HM's, and can defend it
- They name a metric they own or owned, and can explain the inputs to it

Cautious signals:
- Generic answers to "why this company"
- Resume reading instead of synthesis ("In my last role I did X" without explaining why it matters)
- Vague metrics ("a lot of revenue", "significant growth")
- Talking about tools instead of outcomes ("I used Salesforce and Outreach" instead of "I rebuilt the qualification framework and conversion rates moved from X to Y")
- Asking about WFH policy, vacation, or comp before round 2
- Bad-mouthing a previous employer
- Inability to articulate why they're leaving the current role

## The 11pm Launch Test

The internal HM heuristic that almost no one says out loud: "Would I want this person on my team at 11pm during a launch when something is broken."

This is not about being agreeable. The 11pm test is asking:
- Will they figure out what's broken without me telling them
- Will they communicate clearly under stress
- Will they own the problem or look for someone to blame
- Will they still be calm in 4 hours when we're still debugging

Candidates who pass: ones who tell a specific story about a crisis they handled, with the technical and political detail intact. Candidates who fail: ones who have only ever talked about wins, or whose hard-moment stories collapse into "and then we hit our goals."

## What Actually Kills Candidates at the HM Screen

In rough frequency order:

1. **Vague answers to specific questions.** "How did you build that book of business" answered with "I focused on relationships and execution" is a kill.
2. **No business outcome metrics.** Candidates who can't say what they were measured on, or what the number was.
3. **Talking about tools instead of results.** The Salesforce admin who lists 14 platforms but can't explain a single problem they solved.
4. **Asking about comp/benefits in the first 30 minutes.** Signals priority order. Comp questions are appropriate at round 2 or with the recruiter, not the HM screen.
5. **No questions at the end, or only generic ones.** "What does success look like in this role" is a fine warm-up; if it's the only question, that's a problem.
6. **Couldn't articulate why they want this job specifically.** This is the most common kill at the HM stage.

## How to Actually Impress in 30 Minutes

The candidates who get the HM walking out of the screen saying "we have to hire this person" do four things:

1. **Walk in with a hypothesis.** "Based on what I read about the company and what you said in the JD, my guess is the biggest unlock for this role in the next 6 months is X. Am I close?" HMs love this when the hypothesis is reasonable, even if wrong.
2. **Demonstrate the work.** Not "here's what I did," but "here's how I'd think about your problem." Bring a small artifact: a 3-bullet diagnostic, a quick teardown of their pricing page, a hypothesis about why their funnel is leaking. This costs you 30 minutes of prep and is the single highest-use move.
3. **Tell one hard story with the political and technical detail intact.** A real story has a moment where you were wrong, or where the team disagreed, or where the customer escalated. Candidates who scrub those moments out sound like they've never actually shipped anything.
4. **Ask one question the HM didn't expect.** Not "what's the company culture like." Something like "What's the team's current call on whether to build vs buy the X capability you mentioned in the JD" or "Who is the person on your team whose work you'd most want to clone."

## What This Means for the Operator

- **The Cadence Analytics work is the artifact.** When prepping for an HM screen, lead with a specific build (multi-pass scoring, a data-store migration, the imap reply loop). HMs at AI-native companies will recognize that "I built production ML end to end" is a stronger signal than any keyword on a resume.
- **The customer-facing numbers need to be at the front of every resume bullet.** "Global markets" and the deal sizes are the kind of dollar/scale numbers HMs scan for. Don't bury them.
- **Remote-first preference needs a clean, brief script.** When it surfaces, an HM is asking "is this person going to be a logistical headache." A 10-second confident answer ("remote-first, no impact on day-to-day") closes the loop. A long explanation creates more concern than the preference itself.
- **Bring a hypothesis to every HM screen.** For a RevOps role: "I'd guess your current funnel reporting has a gap between marketing-attributed pipeline and sales-accepted pipeline." For an FDE role: "I'd guess your forward deployment work is hitting friction on the implementation handoff." HMs notice this immediately.
- **The "why are you leaving the current build" question may come up.** Have a clean answer that frames the build as a portfolio project, not a competing commitment. Don't apologize for shipping production code on the side; that is the asset, not the liability.
